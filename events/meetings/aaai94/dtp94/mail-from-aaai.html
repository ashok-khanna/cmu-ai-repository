Please give us an electronic mail address that you read on a regular basis. If you need to contact AAAI, you should use sss@aaai.org or fss@aaai.org, as appropriate. The phone number for AAAI is 415 328-3123. Annette Eldredge is the Symposium Coordinator at AAAI. Deadlines: One thing about running several symposia in parallel is that deadlines are very important. To get things done on time, we will have to be strict about all deadlines. In particular, if you miss the deadline for having your call for participation ready, your symposium may be dropped. Call for Participation: Your first duty is to get your symposium committee together and prepare your call for participation. You send us a draft of this call, and we will help revise it if necessary. AAAI will then collect, edit, typeset, proofread, print, and mail out the calls to the entire AAAI membership. So what needs to be done now is for you to decide with your program committee what your Call for Participation should be like. This is a good time to think about how you plan to run the symposium and, if you are going to do something especially creative, to slant your Call appropriately. (See the section on Symposium Activities, below.) In any case, your Call needs to include a title, short description, submission information, and the program committee of your symposium. Don't worry about generic symposium information (like what's a symposium? where? when?) as this will be in the part of the Call that AAAI and I put together. You need to decide what you want people to send you so that you can select attendees. As far as we are concerned, the only requirement is that this material should ensure that you and your committee can fairly review potential attendees. You should think about how much paper you want to sift through and also about what information will be most useful to you in making your decisions. In the end, most of you will probably want abstracts of new or existing papers; but short position statements, full papers, or even CVs are ok, if you and your committee could do a better job with them. Please note that this material can be completely unrelated to the material that winds up in the Working Notes (see below). AAAI has copies of previous calls for participation. If you would like to use one as a guide, AAAI can either mail or FAX one to you. Publicity: The Call for Participation will be sent out to the entire AAAI membership. In addition, we try to hit a few of the major bulletin boards, like comp.ai and news.announce.conferences. However, YOU WILL almost certainly WANT TO SEND OUT TARGETED MAILINGS OF YOUR PARTICULAR CALL TO YOUR FAVORITE MAILING LISTS, BULLETIN BOARDS, AND OTHER GROUPS OF PEOPLE. This is especially important if you think that a large fraction of your potential audience are not AAAI members. Historically, we receive a large number of complaints from people who have forgotten about the deadline by the time that it arrives (or who have misplaced the Call among their many AAAI mailings). It is always a good idea to RESEND/REPOST THESE MAILINGS or otherwise remind people ABOUT A WEEK BEFORE THE SUBMISSION DEADLINE. We suggest that you not issue direct invitations to people you really wish to attend your symposium, but instead contact these people and encourage them to submit material. (This leads to less work on your part getting the schedule complete and getting the working notes together.) Symposium Activities: Most symposia should have more discussion and interaction than conference-style paper presentations. In the past, symposia run like mini-conferences have been less successful than those with at least half of the time devoted to discussion or other open-format items. Some unusual formats that have been tried include: o A set of questions circulated in advance; presenters limited to addressing those questions. o Papers presented by an attendee other than the author. o Break-out sessions where subgroups discuss particular items, then meet to present their conclusions. o In a symposium in which there was little shared background, each session began with a long tutorial talk followed by several 6-minute presentations of research and then open discussion. No matter what format you choose, you should at the very least try to have discussants or discussion periods that are more than just question periods. One way is to have 2 or 3 short talks in a 90 minute session, taking 45 to 50 minutes total; followed by a commentary, whose purpose is to expose discussion topics; and ending with open discussion, led by the commentator. In this scheme, the commentator should not try to bring up new topics, but can try to generalize, or make conclusions. The commentator should get advance copies of the talks, or, preferably, already be familiar with the work of the presenters. You could even dispense with formal presentations entirely, and instead have the commentator summarize the submissions with the authors responding to this summary. (One advantage of this approach is that it will keep the symposium focussed on fewer topics.) If you decide to have a major speaker you should also have a commentator or panel; this is not only an excellent way to lead into a discussion, but it also encourages the speaker to say something worthy of commentary. There is no problem with presenting work-in-progress in the symposia, rather than just finished work, so you could encourage this. Symposium-hopping is discouraged. IF YOU FEEL THAT THERE WILL BE SUBSTANTIAL OVERLAP BETWEEN YOUR SYMPOSIUM AND ANOTHER THAT IS BEING OFFERED AT THE SAME TIME, YOU MIGHT CONSIDER HOLDING ONE OF YOUR SESSIONS JOINTLY. WORKING NOTES AND TECHNICAL REPORTS: The Working Notes are given to participants are for discussion purposes only and will not be distributed beyond the symposium without permission. (AAAI will also distribute attendance lists to participants in your symposium.) Participants may be encouraged to resubmit elsewhere a final version based on whatever they give you. If you wish, you may turn your Working Notes into a AAAI Technical Report, in which case it will continue to be available from AAAI. This option requires that each participant sign a Permission to Distribute Form (available from AAAI; a copy should be mailed to you) BEFORE the working notes are put together. Note that if you choose this option, AUTHORS STILL RETAIN ALL RIGHTS TO THEIR SUBMISSIONS. Because AAAI supports the symposium series, AAAI Press has right of first refusal on any book publications explicitly resulting from symposia. AAAI Press is not interested in publishing the working notes verbatim, but is instead interested in edited collections of papers, including related papers that were not presented at the symposia. We suggest that you contact AAAI Press soon if you want to prepare a book. If AAAI Press takes too long to make a decision, let us know. (AAAI Press may want two decision points, one on the concept, and one on the actual set of papers; this is fine in our view.) YOU ARE RESPONSIBLE FOR COLLECTING THE WORKING NOTES and sending them to AAAI for duplication. The working notes should be double column, with at least 3/4" of white space on all sides of 8 1/2" by 11" paper. (If anyone uses A4 paper, they should not center on the A4 paper, but instead center on an 8 1/2" by 11" area measured from the top left corner of the A4 paper.) You can use the style guides of any of the recent AAAI or IJCAI conferences, as long as they leave the correct amount of white space. The reason for using the double-column format is to put more information on the page---not to imply publication-quality treatment. Each set of working notes should be under 200 double-column pages; you are free to juggle the sizes of various submissions as long as you stay under the total limit. SYMPOSIUM SIZE AND PARTICIPATION: Because the symposia should be oriented towards discussion, the default size for symposia will be about 60 people. Small symposia (40 or fewer attendees), as well as large symposia (about 100 participants), are also possible. There may be some nice smaller rooms available to us, so there is an extra bonus for thinking small. To change from the default size, however, requires, consultation with us and the AAAI office, to ensure that both facilities and logistical support can handle the change. You and we have to select the size that your symposium will be. If you don't say anything, your size will be the default size. You can then select up to 75% of this number of people unless we agree on some larger percentage. The remainder of the participants in your symposium will be selected by AAAI on a FIRST-COME FIRST-SERVED basis, in response to the call for registration. You will be able to find out which of your selected participants have registered, and will be able to make replacements if necessary. At some time AAAI will replace the unregistered selected participants by people from the first-come first-served pool. You can use any criteria to select participants that you wish (except that you should not be arbitrary, and should not exclude people with different viewpoints from that of your committee without good reason), and can even determine the criteria as you read the submissions. You may ask for different things in the working notes but you will have to ride herd on your submitters to ensure that they make appropriate changes. If you have submissions with multiple authors, you can select all authors, a particular author, or several particular authors. Also, and perhaps best, you can select an author but allow substitutions within the group. If you chose this later course you should correspond directly with the submitters, as AAAI will be sending out registration material as if they were for particular authors. AAAI also will not pay for costs related to program committee meetings or such (You should be able to do everything electronically anyway). If you plead particularly hard, AAAI may also waive registration fees for select members of your committee or particularly important participants who would not otherwise be able to come. Plenary Session: At every symposium series we have had an evening plenary session, open to the public, in which each chair talked about their symposium. However, in the past some of the plenary sessions have contained talks that were boring recitations of titles of talks in the symposium. This is not the purpose of the plenary sessions, however--the talks are supposed to present to an audience of AI researchers something that is interesting and presents some of the issues behind the symposium. Unfortunately, preparing an interesting talk like this can be quite hard, especially when only 10 or 15 minutes is alloted. The symposium chairs, who are busy organizing and running their symposia have typically NOT had time to prepare good talks for this event. Thus, although the default in the past has been for the symposium chairs to do this themselves, we are now requiring that you FIND SOMEONE ELSE TO DO IT. The quality of the speaker, as a ``performer'' should be a key consideration -- we'd like these talks to be informative, but also fun. The speakers should try to focus on one or two issues that are related to their symposium. There are other possibilities, such as presenting a controversy or concern that motivated or arose in the symposium. We will need to know who is giving the talk for your symposium about a month before the meeting to give us time to publish the plenary description.
