This is an exciting project that combines state-of-the-art research on autonomous intelligent agents with the fielding of a real application. Candidates should have a Bachelors or Masters degree in Computer Science and have demonstrated the ability to construct and maintain large-scale, leading-edge systems. Candidates must also be able to work well in a cooperative research environment and express their ideas clearly in presentations and in writing. An ideal candidate would have expertise in artificial intelligence, simulation, C/UNIX, X, and Tcl/TK; although candidates with significant expertise in a subset of these areas will be considered. A final consideration is eligibility for a U.S. security clearance. Although the research and development is not itself classified, deployment of the resulting system may require access to classified facilities. USC/ISI -- which is located along the water in Marina del Rey, California (in the Los Angeles area) -- is part of the USC School of Engineering and maintains close academic ties with the Department of Computer Science. USC/ISI is a leading research laboratory in computer science and engineering, with approximately 250 staff members of whom more than 70 have advanced degrees, and more than 40 others are pursuing advanced degrees. ISI provides an exciting environment that encourages publication and the wide dissemination of research results. The Soar project at USC/ISI is situated within the Intelligent Systems Division, an active group of approximately 60 AI researchers, programmers, and students working in such areas as intelligent agents, knowledge representation, natural-language translation, expert systems, knowledge acquisition, machine learning, planning, human-computer interaction, intelligent information integration, constraint satisfaction, education and training. It is also one of the lead sites in an international, multi-disciplinary, community of researchers concerned with developing Soar as the basis for both integrated intelligent systems and a unified theory of cognition.
